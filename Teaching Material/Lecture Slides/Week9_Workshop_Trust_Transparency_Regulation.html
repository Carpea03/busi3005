Hour One · 60 Minutes
Three jurisdictions. One AI system. Different consequences.
You'll work through how the EU AI Act, Singapore's AI Verify framework, and Australia's principles-based approach would each treat the same business AI system. The point isn't to memorise rules. It's to develop the strategic instinct of a leader who knows which rules apply when.
60 min · Frameworks → Scenario → Group Analysis → Debrief

Three approaches. Three philosophies.

Each framework reflects a different bet about how to regulate AI without strangling it. None of them is neutral.

EU AI Act

Mandatory · Risk-tiered

Four risk categories: unacceptable (banned), high (conformity assessment), limited (transparency), minimal (free). Extraterritorial reach: applies to anyone serving EU users.

Bet: certainty unlocks investment.

Singapore AI Verify

Voluntary · Test-based

Eleven testable principles, an open-source toolkit, an industry sandbox. Used by Google, Microsoft, Meta to evidence compliance for procurement and partnership conversations.

Bet: trust accrues to those who prove it.

Australia

Principles + existing law

Eight AI Ethics Principles, the Voluntary AI Safety Standard, and the existing weight of Privacy Act, anti-discrimination, ACL, sector regulators. Mandatory guardrails for high-risk are still in consultation.

Bet: don't regulate ahead of the technology.

Group Activity · Same System, Three Lenses

SightLine: an AI recruitment platform

An Adelaide-based startup screening graduate-job applicants for ~40 mid-tier Australian and EU employers.

What SightLine does

Applicants upload CVs and complete a 20-minute video assessment. SightLine's model scores candidates on "role fit," "communication clarity," and "growth potential." The top 15% are sent to human recruiters.

What you need to decide

Under each framework: what risk tier does this fall under? What obligations are triggered? What changes would the founders have to make? What evidence would they need to produce?

SightLine has no malicious intent. The team is technically competent and ethically thoughtful. That's not enough. Today's workshop is about what is.

What you should be noticing.

Your group answers won't all match. That's the point.

  1. 1Risk classification is judgement, not lookup. The EU Act lists "employment, worker management, access to self-employment" as Annex III high-risk. SightLine sits there. But where does the human reviewer's discretion sit? That changes the obligation structure.
  2. 2Australia is not the loose option. Australian Privacy Act + the Australian Human Rights Commission's automated decision guidance + ACL misleading conduct provisions probably bind SightLine more than the EU Act, because they don't wait for an AI-specific category.
  3. 3Singapore's framework is the one investors and partners ask about. Voluntary doesn't mean optional — it means market-driven. SightLine without an AI Verify report won't close enterprise deals.
  4. 4The frameworks disagree about who is responsible. Provider, deployer, distributor — your group's answer to "who has to act" depends on which lens you used.
Hour Two · 60 Minutes
Audit, don't admire. The five trust dimensions in practice.
Trust isn't something you announce in a press release. It's something you can audit. This hour, you'll run a structured trust audit on a real, currently-deployed AI system using the five-dimension framework: transparency, accountability, fairness, robustness, privacy.
60 min · Framework → Audit → Report-back

Five questions. Five different failure modes.

Each dimension is a discrete audit lens. Score each independently. Weak in one = trust problem regardless of the others.

Transparency

Can a stakeholder understand how the system reaches its outputs? Is there documentation? Are the model's limits stated?

Accountability

If this system causes harm, who is responsible? Is there a named owner? A complaints route? An override path?

Fairness

Has the system been tested across the populations it affects? Are disparate impacts measured and addressed?

Robustness

Does it perform reliably under edge cases, adversarial inputs, distribution shift? What happens when it fails?

Privacy

What personal data is collected, used, retained, shared? Consent path? Data minimisation? Right to access and delete?

The audit habit

For every AI system you'll touch in your career — yours or someone else's — these are the five questions to ask before deployment, not after.

"The system was working as designed. The design was the problem."

Now design the safeguards.

Pair up. Trade pitches. Design two governance interventions for each other's system.

Layer 1 · Hard guardrails

What the system cannot do regardless of input. What's locked. What requires human approval before it executes. Think Robodebt: where would mandatory human review have stopped the disaster?

Layer 2 · Monitoring

What you'd measure continuously. Output drift, complaint rates, demographic disparity in outcomes, override frequency. The dashboard you'd build before launch.

Layer 3 · Stakeholder engagement

Who you'd consult before changes. Who has standing to flag a problem. The complaints route a real customer can find in under 60 seconds.
