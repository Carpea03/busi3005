Mindset
Why This Feels Uncomfortable (And Why That's Essential)
Professional expertise isn't about choosing sides — innovation or criticism, optimism or scepticism. It's about holding both perspectives simultaneously.
The best AI strategists aren't uncritical enthusiasts. They're people who can build powerful AI systems while also asking "what could go wrong?" and "who might this harm?" before deployment.

The discomfort you feel this week? That's growth. Surface learning feels comfortable — absorbing information that confirms what you already believe. Deep learning is uncomfortable because it changes how you think, not just what you know.

The Prompt Template

Copy this. Adapt for each case.
You are a research assistant investigating a historical case of AI or algorithmic decision-making failure. I am studying [CASE NAME] for a university workshop on unintended consequences of AI deployment. Please investigate and structure your response around the following dimensions:

1. Intended outcome — What was the system designed to achieve? Who commissioned it and why?
2. Actual outcome — What happened when it was deployed? Quantify harms where possible.
3. Failure mechanisms — What specifically went wrong? Was it technical, organisational, or both?
4. Stakeholders harmed — Who bore the costs? Were they the same people who made the deployment decisions?
5. Organisational enablers — What decisions, incentives, or oversight failures allowed this to happen?
6. Accountability — Who was held responsible, if anyone? What was the legal or regulatory response?
7. Relevance to 2026 AI deployments — What analogous risks exist in current generative AI systems?

Provide specific dates, named individuals or organisations, and cited sources where possible. Flag any claims you are uncertain about.

For Hour 1 practice run: replace [CASE NAME] with Robodebt (Australia, 2016–2020).

Three competing visions for AI's economic future. Each backed by Nobel laureates, tech leaders, and credible research. Which do you find most persuasive?

This hour is built for discussion. Don't rush. The point isn't to win — it's to genuinely hold positions you disagree with and see what that feels like.

The Debate
Three Competing Visions for AI's Economic Future

AI will transform work but humans remain essential. Hallucination is unfixable. Society needs accountable professionals.
Jensen Huang · Daron Acemoglu · Erik Brynjolfsson

View 1: Powerful but Won't Automate Everything

The Augmentation View

"You're not going to lose your job to an AI, but you're going to lose your job to somebody who uses AI."

— Jensen Huang, NVIDIA CEO, Milken Institute 2025

Core Argument

LLMs are genuinely powerful but structurally limited. Hallucination is mathematically proven unfixable (Xu et al., 2024). LLMs produce homogeneous outputs on open-ended tasks. Society will always need accountable professionals we can litigate against.

Key Evidence

Nobel laureate Acemoglu: AI will automate only ~4.6% of tasks, yielding 1.1–1.6% GDP growth — 5x below Goldman Sachs' 7% estimate. Brynjolfsson: 15% productivity gains, concentrated among least experienced workers.

"They're telling you, 'Oh look at ChatGPT, it passed the bar exam, there's going to be no need for lawyers,' and all that crap, which has nothing to do with reality."

— Daron Acemoglu, Nobel Prize-winning Economist, 2024
View 1 — Evidence Base
The Research Behind the Augmentation View

"Artificial Hivemind" — Jiang et al. (2025)

26,000 open-ended queries. Every LLM converged on identical metaphors ("Time is a river"). Mode collapse means LLMs can't replicate the diversity of human creative thought.

"Trendslop" — Romasanta et al. (HBR, 2026)

15,000+ trials across 7 strategic tensions. LLMs consistently recommended trendy strategies regardless of context. Better prompting improved results by less than 2%.

"Why LLMs Hallucinate" — Kalai et al. (2025)

By OpenAI researchers themselves. Hallucination arises from structural statistical pressures in training — not superficial defects. A systemic issue, not a fixable bug.

Yale Budget Lab (October 2025)

Since ChatGPT's launch, the "broader labor market has not experienced a discernible disruption" from AI. Goldman Sachs' own follow-up: impacts "still negligible."

View 2: AI Will Automate Everything

The Superintelligence View

"My basic prediction is that AI-enabled biology and medicine will allow us to compress the progress that human biologists would have achieved over the next 50-100 years into 5-10 years."

— Dario Amodei, Anthropic CEO, "Machines of Loving Grace," October 2024

Core Argument

Scaling laws prove capabilities improve predictably with compute. AI will match Nobel-level intellect by 2026–2027 (Amodei). Superintelligence within "a few thousand days" (Altman). 50% of entry-level white-collar jobs could vanish within 5 years.

Key Evidence

Inference costs falling 10x every 12 months. Goldman Sachs: AI could boost GDP 7% (~$7 trillion). McKinsey: $2.6–4.4 trillion annually. MIT study: AI can already do 11.7% of US labour market tasks.

"There will come a point where no job is needed. You can have a job if you want a job… but AI will be able to do everything."

— Elon Musk, AI Safety Summit, November 2023

What the AI Builders Are Saying

Dario Amodei — Anthropic

"We, as the producers of this technology, have a duty and an obligation to be honest about what is coming. I don't think this is on people's radar."

Axios, May 2025

View 3: LLMs Are a Dead End

The Architectural Critique

"The path to superintelligence via LLMs is complete bullshit. It's just never going to work."

— Yann LeCun, Turing Award Winner, November 2025

Core Argument

LLMs are statistical pattern-matching, not intelligence. A house cat understands physics better than the largest LLMs — and LeCun's lab proved it: JEPA models develop object permanence from video while LLMs score near chance (Garrido et al., 2025). He left Meta and raised $1.03B to build the alternative.

Key Evidence

EsoLang-Bench (March 2026): LLMs scoring 85–95% on Python drop to 0–11% on equivalent tasks in unfamiliar languages. Apple's GSM-Symbolic: one irrelevant clause drops maths performance up to 65%. Even Ilya Sutskever (OpenAI co-founder): "The era of 'Just Add GPUs' is over."

"LLMs don't actually know what a nationality is… they know what words are and they know which words predict which other words. And that's pretty much it."

— Gary Marcus, NYU Cognitive Scientist, 2025

Assignment 3: Critical Reflective Analysis

30% · Due End of Week 10

Your final assessment asks you to critically reflect on your journey as an AI-capable business professional across all ten weeks.

What It Is

A critical reflective analysis of your evolving understanding of human-AI collaboration. Use your AI Prompt Log as evidence. Demonstrate growth in critical thinking, not just tool proficiency.

What It's Not

Not a summary of what you did each week. Not a review of AI tools. Not a description of course content. It's an analysis of how your thinking changed and what that means for your professional practice.

What "Critical Reflective Writing" Looks Like

Descriptive (avoid): "In Week 3, I learned about AI agents."
Critical (aim for): "My initial assumption that AI agents could operate autonomously was challenged when I discovered the accountability gap — if an agent makes a consequential error, the liability still sits with a human professional. This tension between AI capability and legal accountability fundamentally changed how I evaluate automation opportunities."
