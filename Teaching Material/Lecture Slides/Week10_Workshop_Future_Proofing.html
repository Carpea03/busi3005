Capability claims, hype detection, and what scaling laws actually predict.
A vendor will pitch you something every month for the rest of your career. Today you build the framework for evaluating those pitches: what scaling laws can and can't tell you, where genuine capability ends and theatre begins, and how to do due diligence in 30 minutes when you have 30 minutes.
55 min · Frameworks → Vendor Evaluation → Debrief

What scaling laws actually say. And don't.

Kaplan and the OpenAI scaling work showed that loss falls as a power law with compute, data, and parameters. That's the theorem. Below is what it does not give you.

What scaling laws give you

Predictable performance gains on well-defined benchmarks as compute increases. Reasonable forecasting of when a capability frontier will be crossed if scaling continues. A reason to disbelieve "this is the limit" claims.

What they don't give you

A guarantee that scaling continues at the same rate. A prediction that benchmark performance translates to real-world capability. Anything about which capabilities emerge when. The Sutskever line is the inside admission: returns on raw scale are flattening.

The strategic lesson

Scaling laws give you a velocity, not a destination. Use them to disbelieve plateau claims. Don't use them to underwrite your business plan.

The hype-detection move

When a vendor implies their roadmap is "a scaling problem," ask: what specific benchmark improves predictably with scale? What's the fallback if that benchmark doesn't translate to your use case?

What strong evaluation sounds like.

Patterns that come up across every group that does this seriously.

  1. 1"78% reduction" without denominator is theatre. 78% of what baseline? Over what period? With what error rate accepted? Replicated by which independent customer?
  2. 2"Powered by [frontier model]" tells you nothing about reliability. The frontier model is fine. The application layer, the prompt scaffolding, the retry logic, the human-in-the-loop fallbacks — that's where systems fail.
  3. 3The cheap pilot exists. Run the agent against last quarter's actual transactions. Compare to the human team's outputs. Measure error rate, error type, recoverability of errors. Sample size of one quarter beats every reference customer.
  4. 4The accountability question is the one that breaks pitches. When the agent posts a misclassified transaction that triggers an ATO inquiry, who is named on the demand letter? "The system" is not an answer.
  5. 5"Available now" + "frontier model" is a red flag, not a green light. The frontier model was different last month and will be different next month. What's your version-pinning strategy? What happens to your contracts when the underlying model deprecates?

Two specific gaps. Both already operational.

Week 9 gave you the architecture for governing AI that recommends. Here's what doesn't fit cleanly inside it.

Gap 1

Autonomous agents

Systems that act on the world without per-action human approval. Send the email. Make the booking. Execute the trade. The Week 9 trust dimensions still apply — but accountability collapses when there's no point of human approval in the loop. Robodebt scaled. This scales further.

Gap 2

Synthetic media

Generated images, voices, videos at fidelity humans can't reliably distinguish. Arup lost AU$25M to a deepfake video conference. The trust dimensions framework assumes there's a system to audit. What do you audit when the medium of evidence itself becomes unreliable?

Week 9's frameworks aren't wrong — they're necessary but insufficient. Your job in this hour is to figure out what to add, not what to replace.

The capability that outlasts every tool.

Mollick and the Wharton work both land on the same idea: it's not the specific tool fluency that matters in five years. It's the meta-skill of integrating new tools faster than your peers.

The diagnostic

When a new model drops: how long until you've tested it on a real workflow you care about? Days = adaptive. Months = at risk. Never = displaced.

The discipline

Maintain one workflow you deliberately refactor every quarter using whatever the current best tool is. The workflow is the fixed point. The tool rotates.

The trap

Specialising in a current tool's quirks. Today's prompt engineering tricks for today's models will be obsolete by your graduation. The transferable skill is the integration practice, not the prompt library.
